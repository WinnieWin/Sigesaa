Why northern Ghana

The most climate-exposed part of the country, with the least room to absorb a shock.

Livelihoods here rest almost entirely on one rainy season. When it arrives late, stops early, or turns to flood, the loss lands on households that have no buffer, and it lands hardest on women.

54%of people in northern Ghana live below the poverty line.
6%of arable land is irrigated, the rest waits for rain.
1rainy season a year. No second chance if it fails.
Too much, then too little. The north's problem is not only flood. It is the same year swinging from washed-out farmland in August to dry wells and idle land by February. Both ends of that swing are climate-information problems, and both are what we work on.

What we do

Two things, done properly.

Focus area 01

Climate-and-health early warning

We use climate, weather and satellite data to give communities and health services warning of floods, extreme heat and the disease risks that follow, in time to act.

  • Flood and standing-water mapping from satellite
  • Heat and disease-risk alerts for health workers
  • Warnings written for the people who must act on them
Focus area 02

Climate services for farming and water security

We tell farming families when the rains are likely to come, what to do if they come late, and how to plan for the dry months.

  • Rainfall onset and cessation advice for planting
  • Seasonal and dry-season water outlooks
  • Practical support for women's farming and income groups

How we work

Science that ends in someone's hands.

We do not build our own networks. We work through the people communities already trust: women's groups, farmer groups, community health volunteers, clinics and district services. A forecast that never reaches a farmer or a midwife has done nothing. Our work is only finished when the information has been used.

Read the data

We do the science ourselves

Satellite and climate data, processed and validated by people who do this professionally, not repackaged from someone else's bulletin.

Translate it

Into a decision, not a chart

Plant now or wait. Move the animals. Watch for fever after the flood. Check on the elderly this week. Treat this water. Plain guidance, in the languages people use.

Deliver it

Through people communities trust

Farmer groups and agriculture extension for the planting side; clinics, community health volunteers and district health teams for the health side. The channels that already exist and already work.

Check it

Ask whether it helped

We go back to the farmers and the health workers and ask if the warning arrived in time, if it made sense, and what we got wrong. Then we fix it.

Who we are

Rooted in the north. Fluent in the science.

Plenty of organisations work in northern Ghana. Very few can read a satellite image, build a forecast and stand in the community it is meant to serve. Sigesaa was founded in Bolgatanga, in the Upper East, to be both.

"I grew up with this rainfall. I now study it for a living. Sigesaa exists to close the distance between what the data already shows and what a family in the north gets told in time to act on."

Dr Winifred Ayinpogbilla Atiah · Founder
